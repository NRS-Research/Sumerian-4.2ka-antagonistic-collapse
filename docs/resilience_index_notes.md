# System Resilience Index W(t) for Sumer: Construction Notes

## 1. Original Data Sources
This index is synthesized from two complementary, authoritative studies of Sumerian settlement systems:
1. **Adams, R. McC. (1981)** *Heartland of Cities: Surveys of Ancient Settlement and Land Use on the Central Floodplain of the Euphrates*. University of Chicago Press.
   - The foundational regional settlement survey of southern Mesopotamia, providing on-ground estimates of settled area, site size, and occupation phases for the entire 3rd millennium BCE.
2. **Wilkinson, T. J., Gibson, M. & Widell, M. (2013)** *Models of Mesopotamian Landscapes: How Small-Scale Processes Contributed to the Growth of Early Civilizations*. Oxbow Books.
   - An integrated landscape model that cross-validates Adams’ survey data with hydrological and agricultural productivity data, refining the chronology of settlement expansion and abandonment.

## 2. Index Construction Methodology
Quantification follows the uniform rules defined in Section S2.1 of the Supplementary Information:
1. **Proxy definition**: Total settled area of major Sumerian urban centers is used as the primary proxy for systemic resilience, as it reflects the system’s ability to sustain concentrated population, cooperative infrastructure, and institutional complexity.
2. **Temporal aggregation**: Settlement occupation phases are aggregated into 50-year time slices, consistent with the chronological resolution of the climate stress index and antagonistic investment index.
3. **Normalization**: Min-max normalization to the [0, 1] interval, where 1 represents the peak settled area during the Akkadian imperial period and 0 represents the pre-urban baseline of the mid-3rd millennium BCE.
4. **Cross-validation**: Settlement area trends are corroborated by independent proxies including demographic estimates derived from site size and stratigraphic evidence of occupation continuity/abandonment.

## 3. Stage-by-Stage Trajectory Explanation
### 3.1 Growth and Peak (2500 – 2300 BCE)
- 2500–2350 BCE: Progressive urbanization of Sumerian city-states, with steady growth in settled area and population density through the Early Dynastic period.
- 2300 BCE: Peak systemic resilience under Akkadian imperial unification. Political integration reduced inter-city warfare and coordinated large-scale irrigation infrastructure, maximizing the effective carrying capacity of the alluvial plain.

### 3.2 Decline and Collapse (2250 – 2050 BCE)
- 2250 BCE: Onset of gradual decline. Marginal settlements in the northern dry-farming zone were abandoned first as aridity intensified, while core urban centers remained intact.
- 2200–2150 BCE: Accelerated contraction following the aridity peak. Weakening imperial control and rising inter-polity conflict eroded systemic capacity, culminating in the fall of the Akkadian Empire.
- 2070 BCE (within the 2050 BCE slice): Collapse of the classic Sumerian urban system. Settled area returns to near-baseline levels, marked by widespread site abandonment, population dispersal, and breakdown of regional institutional structures. This point marks the completion of the climate → antagonism → resilience depletion cascade.

### 3.3 Post-Collapse Recovery (2000 – 1900 BCE)
- Gradual re-urbanization under the Ur III dynasty, with settlement system stabilizing at a lower equilibrium level than the Akkadian peak.

## 5. Limitations and Caveats
1. **Settlement area as a proxy**: Total settled area reflects relative systemic resilience rather than absolute population or economic output. Variations in site density and occupation intensity are not fully captured.
2. **Regional averaging**: The index represents an aggregate trend for the southern Mesopotamian alluvium; individual city-states experienced abandonment at slightly different times.
3. **Chronological uncertainty**: Dating of occupation phases is based on ceramic seriation and associated royal chronologies, with a typical uncertainty of ±30–50 years, consistent with the 50-year aggregation window.
